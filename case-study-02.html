Product Design for an AI-driven Content Creation Hub

UX UI DESIGN

Role

UX UI Designer

Team

Product Owner, Engineers, UX Designer

Duration

1 month

Running a niche website marketing service, my client was looking to develop a website builder derived from their current software. Our primary objective was to design a website builder tailored for churches, allowing them to effortlessly create and manage professional websites without requiring coding or design skills.

Our main goal was to create an industry-specific website builder, enabling easy creation and management of professional websites without coding or design expertise.

I remotely collaborated with a cross-functional team in Seattle, overseeing all aspects of design—from defining the product's functionality to shaping its appearance and user experience.

Discovery & Planning

I led remote sessions for stakeholder interviews and collaborative workshops to elicit and refine project requirements.

Following remote discovery interviews with the Product Owner, we discerned that our user base would be diverse, but our primary focus would be on Small and Medium-sized Business (SMB) owners requiring a cost-effective tool for marketing content creation. To align our efforts, I delved into understanding the business goals in order to formulate a pertinent strategy plan.

Create an app for SMBs to produce cost-effective, compelling content.

Key business goals

∙Expand client base and attract new users

∙Create subscription plans to generate sustainable revenue

∙Leverage expert AI prompt creation knowledge

∙Offer a competitively priced service

∙Ability to be used internally

With expert knowledge from developers we discussed system & technical requirements.

Initially, the project began at a high level due to the absence of a definitive product definition. I conducted extensive research on various products and subsequently collaborated with the team to precisely determine our project goals over time. Given the agile nature of development, flexibility and adaptability to changes in requirements were crucial.

UX Reseach

Competitor Research

At the start of our project, I looked into what other companies are doing in the same space. I evaluated their strengths and weaknesses to understand the competition better. This research helped me make informed choices for our product to stand out in the market.

My research began by exploring AI-driven content creation tools like Speechform and Grammarly. Considering our project's dual role as an editor/creator and file storage, akin to Dropbox and Google Drive, I then delved into CMS platforms such as CMS Hub, WordPress, Shopify, and others. In this space, I identified potential competitors like ClickUp, Narrato Jasper, and Copy.ai, which focus on both content creation and CMS functionality. Our unique approach combines advanced content creation tools with a comprehensive CMS repository, distinguishing us in this competitive landscape.

This knowledge would help me to begin defining the product for discussion with the team.

Gathering this information enabled me to envision the positioning of our product within the competitive landscape. It also provided valuable insights into prevalent and effective UX concepts employed by other products. This knowledge is instrumental in crafting a design that is not only user-friendly but also familiar to our users, facilitating ease of learning and use.

What exactly are we making?

Having gained an understanding of existing products, I returned to the team to precisely define our project, both in technical terms and in its actual manifestation. We deliberated on whether it would take the form of a CMS, a Content Platform, or a Content Repository, perhaps even adopting a Headless CMS model akin to Contentful.

SYNTHESIZE & DEFINE

Based on our findings, I created proto personas, outlined problem statements, and developed user stories.

Key user needs

∙Struggle to maintain a consistent tone

UX DESIGN

I started by structuring the system for a clear and user-friendly information flow, focusing on logical taxonomy and user mental models.

Organizing and categorizing information for user clarity while ensuring consistency across the product presented a challenge. I tackled this by proposing different configurations using sitemaps and user flows. The aim was to maintain clear categories and establish cohesive labeling and taxonomy for a well-defined mental model.

Types of questions we needed to figure out as a team:

— What do we call a document that they are editing and compiling?

— Do we use folders concept like Google Drive or another conceptual model?

— What folder and storage naming conventions make sense?

Wireframes & Walkthroughs

With an understanding of the system structure and core functionality, I could begin sketching ideas for the layout and user experience. I kept them rough so we could quickly eliminate unwanted ideas and promptly experiment with new ones.

Testing out different type of site layouts, interactions and animations which would best suit our product and user needs, and match our tech stack.

We tested out layout and naming concepts with paper and then lo-fi prototypes.

During this phase, we utilized user stories to test prototypes, ensuring users could effortlessly perform key tasks as defined. We also brainstormed various naming conventions and aimed to establish a consistent tone for the product.

After deciding on a design concept, I created a low-fidelity interactive prototype to systematically assess each section of the system. This ensured alignment with user and Product Owner needs, with developers providing feedback through demo reviews.

VISUAL AND UI DESIGN

I opted for a strategic design approach utilizing minimalism and rapid prototyping.

With no explicit branding instructions but a very tight deadline, I opted for a neutral and minimalist design, avoiding over-design. I chose simple grays to maintain focus on the core task of content creation.

Given the time constraints, I swiftly transitioned the low-fidelity designs into medium/high-fidelity prototypes, which were subsequently approved by the Product Owner.

Getting the designs ready for handover

Developers were provided with XD prototypes that included design specs, a component library, and exportable elements. User flows for essential tasks, as outlined in user stories, were clearly defined, facilitating a precise understanding of the user journey for developers. Additionally, animations and key interactions were communicated through comments within XD.

TESTING & VALIDATION

Testing with the team

We conducted frequent testing of XD prototypes, actively engaging the development team and Product Owner. This iterative process facilitated the integration of valuable feedback, ensuring alignment with expectations. Given the ongoing development phase, testing and validation were confined to in-house users and stakeholders.

TAKEAWAY

Taking the initiative can be important

Currently, the product is still in development as the team had to prioritize other projects. However, the components that have been developed are already in use internally.

Up to now, the time-on-task and task success rates have significantly enhanced the experiences of in-house developers and designers. This improvement is attributed to both upgraded technologies and enhanced usability of the components.

This project has emphasized the significance of balancing business requirements and user needs. It also taught me the importance of taking initiative, especially when a client is very busy running their business.
